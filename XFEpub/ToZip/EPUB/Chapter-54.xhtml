“There is…one,” Derosa sighs, rubbing at her temples as she sits behind her monolithic desk, a dozen servo-skulls darting around carrying parchment and pens for her to look at and sign. “Though he is a boorish individual.”

“Is that so, Governor?” you ask politely, standing of course as none of her furniture could possibly handle an armored space marine.

“He was convinced that we were to be married, once upon a time. Then his father died, he became the head of his House and I…well,” she smiles slightly, “I’ve become a Governor over an entire world.”

The name of the Rogue Trader who, as of yet, only rarely comes around Subsector Aurelia since Derosa snubbed his advances so long ago, is Claudius Alexandre Jullien Tarquindil. Of the House Tarquindil. Segmentum Ultima, as the least-settled of all the Segmentum by the Imperium, is rife with opportunity for one such as he who needs not follow many an Imperial law as he goes about his business. His business is largely, it seems, both rediscovering old human settlements to bring them the light of the Imperium, selling them technology at massively unfair prices for their ignorance, as well as perhaps dangerously diving into the grey market of xenos trading, both flesh and technology. Only the most exotic of things interest him, and yet he is more than a little mercurial. A perceived insult from a planetary governor a decade ago saw him abandon the entire Sector that the planet lied in forever from his trading routes.

It is, of course, wholly up to you as to whether or not you wish to speak with him in the first place.

==========================================​

“Gabriel,” Adrastia hums as she looks you over. “I think that Subsector Aurelia has seen more Inquisitorial attention in a single century that some Sectors see in generations.”

“At the least this should be a more positive interaction,” you reply, though there is little humor in your voice.

“I should hope so. It would be a terrible shame for the Imperium if you’d called me here to report yet more issues amongst the Blood Ravens,” she drawls.

Even now, upon the Omnia Arcanum, she looks wholly unconcerned about making conversation involving the potential utter destruction of the Chapter. Then again, you had seen her match even Chaos Space Marines blow for blow with a power sword despite her exterior appearance. It was incredibly likely that she had many an enhancement that only an Inquisitor could afford to possess.

“It is, in fact, a gift for you,” you gesture behind you where Martellus slowly unfurls the cloth covering the Fusils.

Adrastia is on her feet and moving faster than you could catch before she is halfway towards the weapons, her ever present inferno pistol still flickering at her side.

“Hrud Fusils,” she hisses, and there is unmistakable interest and want in her voice as she runs her fingers over the weapons. “I had wondered if it might be possible to procure such in the aftermath of where I suspect you got these, but the Mechanicus got there first,” she pouts slightly before stepping back, eyes flicking to you. “They destroyed them all for being techno-heresy, or so they say. And yet you possess them.”

“Indeed. I have heard well that those of the Inquisition find them useful, while we of the Blood Ravens…,”

“Would do well to avoid open usage of such things considering the eyes upon the Chapter,” she flicks her hands away in casual dismissal, “Yes, yes I am aware. I am also aware that such a gift would not be given freely, would it.”

You consider it, and shake your head.

“Indeed.”

“Well,” she hums, tapping her finger alongside one, and then juts her chin out at her phalanx of black-armored troopers with the Inquisition’s symbol stamped in silver on their shoulders and helmets. “I wonder.”

One of them steps forward, silent as the rest of his brethren, and is promptly handed one of the weapons.

“Do you think you could use it, Merrick?” she tilts her head even as Martellus jerks slightly.

The stormtrooper takes off their helmet, to reveal the grizzled face of the alive Colonel Merrick, who lifts his chin at you in silent greetings before putting the weapon’s stock against his shoulder.

“Feels funny, but I figure I could use it, yeah.”

“Excellent,” Adrastia murmurs before looking at you, then at Martellus. “The identity of my retinue is, of course, classified in the extreme, yes?”

“She stole me off the battlefield, middle of a heart attack and kidney failure at the same time, and uh,” Merrick interjects, shrugging at his own remarkably youthful appearance, “Made me young again.”

“How could I not desire a soldier who has stared hive tyrants in the face without faltering, who defended against his own sworn brothers in blood when Vandis and Chaos came calling?”

In the end, of course, despite your surprise, Adrastia accepts your gifts without further questions though no doubt she has her suspicions. It is not the most incredible of things you could have given her, but she acknowledges your aid and the weapons well enough. She exits to a ship that could have been just a jet black strike cruiser, given the profile, except for the fact that it was missing a bombardment cannon, drop pod exits, and more that had instead been replaced with lance weaponry, torpedo bays, and likely much more weaponry and technologies that you could not even precisely see. The ink black vessel, only the massive silver symbol of its allegiance marking it, soon after disappears in the warp leaving you with only more questions and paranoia than you had before.

So, in the end, the usual for an encounter with an Inquisitor such as Adrastia.

The combat servitor blurted out a death oath to the Omnissiah in binary cant before it exploded, unfortunately ineffectually. Which, in this case, amounted to merely exploding a few orks rather than its main target. Shattered metal and sparking circuitry joined with the many, many piles of the rest of its kin, while the orks present toasted themselves with fungus rum, one even going so far as to light up a cigar as the congratulated themselves on another successful fight. The fact that every day their numbers dwindled more and more was happily ignored for the moment.

Of course, this changed when they heard the telltale noises of others coming through the single passage that remained into the former loading bay. Once it had looked out into the void, before collected rock and metal of a dozen ships and more had been crushed together in the physics-defying nature of the Warp for centuries and centuries on end.

“Aw, c’mon,” Bluddflag moaned, dragging himself to his feet once more, adjusting his hat. “More of you mekbo- oh ho! Space marines!” his voice lightened as he spoke, and the ork grunted as he raised his kutlass into a welcoming salute as the stomping and unmistakable suits of the beakies arrived before him.

“Bluddflag,” the leader amongst them called out, “The Blood Ravens have come for you.”

The Kaptin shrugged, and brushed off his shoulders with one hand before picking up his mega-shoota.

“You can give it a go, if you want. I’ll warn ya though, space marine,” he pointed at them with the kutlass, “I’ze killed yer kind before, spikey and un-spikey types.”

“Unfortunately for you, ork,” their leader said, before raising a hand and twitching its fingers, “I have learned more than enough from my time in the Deathwatch of one of the best ways to deal with your kind when in situations like this.”

“Oh yeah? How?”

“Like this,” Hamul grunted, and clenched his fist.

At which point, orks simply disappeared as seven assault cannons and ten cyclone missile launchers fired at once. Cover simply ceased to be, ancient metal deck plates torn up for defensive cover, scrap metal of long rusted and ruined aircraft and vehicles, and general detritus and debris alike all were obliterated completely and utterly. Terminators strode forward and slammed down storm shields, forming an impromptu shield wall, while their brothers continued to fire near endlessly. Only when the ammo for the assault cannons and missile launchers actually ran dry in an exorbitant display of firepower did they cease.

“Hurr,” Bluddflag staggered out of the inferno and to his knees, before weakly gesturing with his kutlass as he bled from over a hundred new holes in his chest, one leg gone entirely and his shoota-arm similarly torn apart. His hat was entirely non-existent save for a few scraps of black leather and cloth. “G-get ‘em boyz….show ‘em…what for…,” the Kaptain gurgled, one assault round having ripped through his throat and out the other side, exposing meat and spine to the open air.

Out of the still lifting smoke charged those few nobs that had survived by virtue of being the furthest back and the sheer weight of bodies in front of them. In turn, the assault terminators lifted their shields and engaged with their hammers, Hamul striding forth ahead of all of them straight towards Bluddflag. Uther’s own cries to the Emperor boomed out through the lonely halls of the Judgement of Carrion, where they would echo forever more long after the Chaplain had departed. More orks arrived, out of the ducts and having heard the signs of battle, only to be targeted by now reloaded cannons and launchers, while those who got close enough tasted fiery death.

“Dat…wasn’t…very sporting of you…beakie,” Bluddflag gasped up at Hamul, even managing to swing with some considerable force with his weapon only for the Champion to take a step back so that the power-field insured that it stabbed itself deep into the metal floor and stuck itself there.

“Orks have no honor. I will show them none. A lesson of the Deathwatch, ork,” the space marine noted calmly before rising up with his boot and then bringing it down onto the skull of the Kaptin at a resolute and steady pace, until there was nothing but wet meat and bone chips remaining.

“Why could the Mechanicus not deal with him themselves?” one of his brother spoke, one he knew not but who was of the Honor Guard, “That was no great battle.”

“They were busy with the madness of this place, and the vast amounts of tyranids and daemons that remain in it even now,” Uther answered for Hamul who himself had been curious. “They kept him contained fine enough, and would have gotten to him eventually.”

“I see. In any case, our task is done, let us-,” Hamul twisted at astonishing speed for one clad in Terminator-armor and grabbed a leaping lictor out of mid-air and slammed it down, lighting claws flashing as he tore it in half.

“Let us leave here, and return to the Chapter.”

“You are, all of you, now my Scout Marines,” Cyrus barked as he stalked back and forth across the deck.

It was not quite a stage, but rather a raised platform intended for just such a purpose as a near parade ground was constructed just so that the 10th​ Company could be taught and drilled as needed. Behind Cyrus stood the silent frames of the 10th​ Company, their armor recolored and heraldries changed to reflect their new positions. Some had been sergeants, others had not been, all were now here, silent and imposing behind him as he glared down at all fifty of his new recruits.

“You will fight. You will kill. Some of you,” he glanced at them, finding weakpoints already, those who were still struggling to make the full mental transition into a space marine, “Will die. But know this, even in death, you will serve the Emperor with your actions.”

He then stabbed a finger straight down, towards the planet below.

“Your first test, shall be to once more be reacquainted with what you once were, and so that they can see what they may someday be.”

All across the Chapter, some together, some ships far apart, did the Blood Ravens drill and train. Some for what was coming soon, in a battle with the vilest of xenos, and others simply so that they had the chance to become Blood Ravens in the first place.

“Damn it.”

Martellus glared at the ruined piece of once-beautiful machinery. How hard could it possibly be, he had thought? Well, now he had learned. At the least, he had learned.

“I will not let your sacrifice be in vain, my friend,” he patted the heap of dissembled technology, and sighed before fiddling with another piece that squawked at him once before falling silent.

Saddened, he turned back to the forge itself surrounding him. Perhaps if he had just had better tools, a better facility? At the last it would help him from lamenting over the loss of a rapier once more, for surely if he simply did better its secrets would be fully revealed to him. One day, he would fix what he had inadvertently broken in his research, overeager as it had been.

Of course, first he needed to peruse the missives that the Mechanicus had sent. A list of things that could be done in exchange for the ships that the Chapter needed to rebuild…

“I can’t do it, I’m sorry,” Nero growls, blood pouring from his empty eye sockets. “They are eldar, if not of the Craftworlds. They are more adept at concealing themselves from sight through the materium and immaterium alike than orks or most cultists.”

“The whole of the Librarius has turned towards this,” Justinius turns towards you, blood trickling from one of his own eyes as well. “And we are finding ourselves stymied. One of the Lexicani was devoured from within after he peered too deeply into the warp, trying to find them.”

It is more than a little frustrating, to find even the psychic might of the Blood Ravens incapable of finding the foe in this instance. It is not as if it is impossible, for the warp is utterly unpredictable and cannot be trusted more often than not. It would have to be the eldar, regardless of the designation, that would be such an annoyance to what is still otherwise a long refined and trustworthy talent of your Librarius.

“There is another option,” Apollo suddenly speaks from beside you, drawing the attention of all who stand before the blazing beacon at the heart of the Litany of Fury.

“Apollo?”

“They are predators. We know, at the least, that they have at least four of their cruisers at the anchorage, though nothing about what remains inside. But they are eager for pain and torture, hunting,” he grunts before running a hand over his scalp. “They could be baited.”

It is a dangerous gamble.

One strike cruiser, deliberately made to appear as wounded, to draw out the Eldar. Then, once the Dark Eldar arrived, could you arrive in force and attack them. Those that fled, you would chase after instead of being forced to flee as Cyrus did before, and let them bring you to their Anchorage. It is either that, or once more attempt to have the Librarians pierce through their own anti-psychic defenses once more.
