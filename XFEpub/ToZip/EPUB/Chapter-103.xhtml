Tertium’s Holding
100 vs. 38

The red giant breathed heavily from where it stood atop the bodies. The smaller red ones loitered about by it. Each step was heavy with sticky gore, crunching shattered guns and heavy weaponry beneath their feet. With one hand it loosely held a sparking crozius arcanum, larger than any other carried in the chapter. In the other it held an ork by the throat. An especially large specimen, who snarled weakly in the grip before the fingers tightened and bone within was suddenly forced without. One had to squint, at this point, to see the floor of guns beneath the new layer of bodies. Or the layer of bodies on top of that. The rapiers and dreadnoughts had punished all who had sought to swarm, while the tight single core of chaplains had ensured every attempted breakthrough had failed. All while the techmarines had busied themselves in the very center of the formation.

Those of the 1st​ Company thumped about, tracking back and forth from within their terminator armor. But for a moment, it was unneeded.

Hannibal Palrun, gore-strewn, clad in the crimson of his enemies, tightened his grip on his crozius and slammed it down on the ork’s head. The power fields erupted, exploding the upper torso entirely and sending limbs flying. At least a third of his power armor was gone at this point, revealing dirt-brown tribal-marked flesh in arms thick with muscle, a spots of a torso covered in scars. One of the last marines to ever be recruited from Typhon Primaris had been fighting orks since he was born. But never had he extorted such a toll. And though Typhon Primaris was now a truly dead world, some quiet and hidden part of him hoped that he had honored his ancestors with the amount of dead in the here and now.

But then there was the sound of bolter fire. Heavy bolters. Shouts of fury and hate. The crack-boom of a thunder hammer and power mace. Slowly, the whole of battlegroup tertium turned to face the newest hallway. The orks had been relentless, attacking from at least three different directions at once with varying levels of intensity that ran from high to unbearable. The tunnels they came from changed seemingly by the minute as each individual mob was chivvied into place by Doom-Eater. Until stopping for the past blessed sixty seconds at least. The sounds of fighting increased in volume and intensity further and further until it came to a halt.

“Chapter Master,” Hannibal greeted the leader of all Blood Ravens as he turned the corner.

Thumps of metal feet and the clang of axes and swords against the walls undercut it all. But more than anything was the classic scream of their kind regardless of time and place.

“WAAAAAAAAGGH!!”

Hestior looked at Calus and Daedal who were by now shouting at one another. Then at the rest of the marines present. Technically, there was no truly senior techmarine present. A part of the wall, or rather one section of the walls with several missing portions of its surface, was the target. Wildly sparking wires, shattered and otherwise damaged speakers, and the corpses of five mega-nobs.

Holding And Receiving
97 vs. 69
74 vs. 88

Cyrus, of course, had heard the order. But he had not followed through, not yet, when he’d heard of the techmarine’s plan. The scouts could not be afforded, nor could the neophytes, for recklessness unless absolutely necessary. So they had held position. The orks had seemed busy elsewhere anyhow, turning about to stream, presumably, in the direction of the Chapter Master. The loudest and most vicious targets. Who had, he also needed to presume, been purposefully antagonizing the foe. As had been planned. But considering the fact that those under his watchful had performed as they had, he felt…satisfied, if he was being honest.

Pride, though he would never admit it.

A rippling cascade of planted explosives, mines, detonator packs, and more, planted in the middle of the battle. Something that he would have, he knew, only ever trusted himself to manage. Or perhaps his best scouts. But, led by the 1st​ Company marines, masquerading as mere scout sergeants, a great many squads had performed well. Very well. Some might even be up for actual promotion after this fight. Might. Maybe. Probably not. In any case, he was still waiting. Patience was difficult for some to maintain amidst a fight, but not for him. He had plenty of sluggish targets on one hand, and his rifle with the other.

Though the new arrival was making things interesting as well. Or, at least, impending arrival. He had said nothing to his scouts, desiring to see their reactions honestly, but that did not mean he couldn’t listen through the command vox channels.

“Push forward, brothers!”

Marcus Crixus was an…interesting choice for Captain of the 1st​ Company. In terms of veterans, it should not have been him. Either Cyrus, or Tarkus, perhaps. But no, they had been destined for other things, and despite everything, the young Captain had acquitted himself well. The fact that the 1st​ Company still existed at all was evidence enough of that. He’d earned his place. By sheer dint of surviving what he had if nothing else. His voice, thread and hoarse, was amplified rather purposefully by his helm, was the only sign that could be detected for his scars. Phosphex was a fearsome thing indeed. And now, pouring blood from his chest and legs, he threw aside a painboy to be caught by some of his other marines as he finally intruded upon the bay.

The reactions from his scouts was about as expected. Some cheered wildly and rose up from behind cover. Foolish, and he made a note to reprimand them later. Others stuttered out their firing accuracy, distracted by the new arrival and failing to make proper accommodations to fire upon those greenskins turning about to fire upon the newcomers. Some, however, he noted, did as was best. They modified their target choices, shifted position to ensure maximum effectiveness, or otherwise turned to aid their fellows who had already been injured or otherwise. A scant minority focused their attentions on those who had failed this impromptu test, showing remarkable initiative and possible command leanings. Another note, to speak to those he noticed.

“Bring those injured along, I will not leave our brothers behind,” Marcus rasped, hefting a battle brother to their feet. “If they must be kept from combat, we will do so in an area that they can properly defend.”

The orks flushed from the bay, retreating as the new arrivals and those already present cut apart the current attacking force from two directions. But they would be back. They would always be back.

For a moment, Cyrus paused, and considered Thaddeus’ words from the Aurelian Crusades.

And then dismissed them. Such earnest hope had been burnt out of him two centuries ago.

For now, as ever, there was only war.

“Rally and regroup, initiates,” Cyrus broadcast. “Ready yourselves to break from the bay and head inwards. Our mission is to find and eliminate Doom-Eater himself.”

Marcus pulled his blade from the guts of another ork and nodded to him. Of the scarred ruin within there was no sign, his helm still kept securely in place. Cyrus wasn’t sure if it was vanity, that he kept himself covered at nearly all times, but he wouldn’t speak against it here and now. Even if he was of the opinion that space marines should be capable of examining the sight of the price the galaxy demanded of the Emperor’s space marines.

“Cyrus,” the Captain nodded to his elder, “Good to see you and the 10th​ Company is well.”

“As well as they can be,” Cyrus shrugged, making sure to keep an eye out on the entrances to the bay. “We have taken casualties, and there will be dead to be reclaimed later on.”

There was not much else to say to each other, Cyrus too taciturn and Marcus too quiet, but there was not much that needed to be said either. For there came a rumbling and thumping, a staccato of primitive weaponry firing, all the while the cry of the orks began to echo once again from the tunnels and walls. Orders needed to be given, brothers granted as much aid as the apothecaries could manage. The techmarines, of course, were busy with something else, communicating solely with their brothers across the superstructure in other battlegroups. Even now, they’d begun attaching stolen cables into the walls, even ripping of the speakers off its mooring and jamming their hands into it. A small cloud of servo-skulls surrounded them as they worked.

“Stoopid beakies! Don’t you lot know when yer out-kunnined? Getting togever like dat just makes it easier ta dakka ya! Bigger targets mean a better foight! Now get in dere boyz, and dakka dem! And leave the shiny gubbins for da meks, we’ze gonna needz ‘em ta fix up me hallways n’such!”

Heavy clomping feet announced the arrival of Tomb-Brother Cassius, his hull somewhat pitted and scarred after a series of explosions from an entire herd of sort of acid-blooded squig. Though many had died before getting close enough to be properly dangerous, the sheer numbers had proven to be their greatest advantage. Not enough to slay the entombed revenant, but the damage was still annoying for Cassius to deal with.

“I have been alive for three and a half centuries, and never have I heard such an annoying ork,” he posited before pivoting to pick up and throw a piece of rusted metal to bisect the first of the ork’s pouring out of the tunnels.

“Annoying?! You stupid fat killa-kan stompy little grot! I’ll show you annoying! I’ll-,”

Blessedly, and with the thanks to the Omnissiah going up from all of the techmarines, there was a sudden sparking and blinding electrical eruptions from the walls. Speakers, clearly put in place obsessively to enforce the orders of Doom-Eater, began to explode after releasing massive sonic booms that rattled them in their frames. Just as was occurring elsewhere, with only a few seconds of difference, the disruption pulse sent orks to their feet. Cybernetic implants that burned with pain and allowed the roars of Doom-Eater passage not just through metal but meat suddenly sparked where they were lodged in thick skulls and painfully adjusted vertebrae.

And, best of all, the short-circuiting had resulted in the complete destruction of the sound system.

There was nothing to be heard from Doom-Eater.

“My ears once burned with phosphex, down to the bone,” Marcus commented as the combined battlegroups began to shift into one and head towards the hallways. “Yet his voice continues to grate at them as if they were never gone. I shall thank him for that when I find him.”

“If I do not find him first,” Cyrus glanced at him.

“Best of luck,” Marcus nodded as they all began to move.

“I don’t believe in luck.”

Starborne Warfare

Shattered wrecks slowly tumbled through the void. Howling voices that once guffawed and jeered with every motion and rattling boom of their cannons were silent. The Potentia, mightiest of the 4th​ Company Fleet, and one of the remaining battle barges of the Blood Ravens, hung surrounded by the corpses of a great many vessels. It bled, heavily, but its burning still blazed defiantly within its adamantium skin. Carefully, achingly slow, the rest of the Chapter Fleet peeled back the self-made cocoon. Various experimental fuel mixtures still leaked into the void, the ships that once made use of them cracked and broken. With pushes of purposefully reduced yields in explosions, the tugging of thunderhawks with attached clamps, the battle-scarred hulk was once more exposed to distant starlight. Potentia’s bombardment cannon was bent, but such damage was not difficult to repair.

Deep within, its master stalked through blackened corridors scored by slug throwers and the exotic weaponry only orks could craft in their maddened minds. A dozen wounds that would be considered severe even for space marine physiology struggled to clot together. A mixture of melted slag and vaporized bone was all that remained of his left arm past the shoulder, a mek’s strange melta cannon misfiring as it died striking the blow. All around them, the corpses of dead servitors and serfs lay amongst the bodies of the orks who had come to kill them all. Despite the entreaties of Nero Gracchus, Hadrian Priscus refused to be remanded to the apothecarium as had many of his company. Some would require healing for some time, others perhaps some bionic replacement, while three would require dreadnought placement. Many tarantula sentry turrets had been damaged, but their ability to hold entire corridors had proved invaluable. Even now they were undergoing repairs.

“Captain, please, your primary heart is in its third set of cardiac arrest, and your Larraman’s is struggling to pick up the slack of the rest of your wounds,” the apothecary pleaded, his white armor liberally splattered with those who he had killed in the name of preserving his brothers.

“Not…yet,” Hadrian snarled, stomping forward tirelessly even as multiple squads moved about on the ship, their movements reported through the company-wide vox.

The Chief Apothecary sighed.

“At least let me take the metal shard out of your skull?”

Hadrian paused, only for a moment, and turned slightly so that the foot-long spike of metal that had just barely penetrated through his skull – only to miss the brain by a centimeter – could be torn out of both his head and helmet.

“There, are you happy now?” Hadrian sniffed, though his rolling eyes were hidden behind the lenses of his helm.

“Well no, the fighting has been over for an hour now and you haven’t actually allowed me to get to the business of healing you, so…,” Nero trailed off as an ork rose from the piles of its dead.

Roaring wordlessly, a reloaded kustom kannon whirring as it spun up in its hands, the nob managed to fire fifty full rounds before Hadrian reached it. Blood practically spat out of his back as the rounds went through his already compromised rib plates. In response, Hadrian reached forward with his remaining hand and wrapped it around the barrel of the kannon, gritting his teeth as he forced it upwards and against the ork’s skull. A trail of rounds continued to spit into the ceiling for the first strike, and the second, before beginning to slack for the third and fourth. It stopped entirely by the sixth. Hadrian, on the other hand, did not stop until the tenth. By then the nob, the last ork alive from the screaming fleet that had come charging in, gleeful to attend to Hadrian’s broadcasted challenge that they come for a real fight, was long dead. Brain and bone covered the wall, slowly scorching black as the cheery red heat from the barrel rested against the pulp.

Only then did the Captain of the 4th​ Fleet turn around and face Nero as he pulled off his helmet and let it drop to the floor.

“There. Now it’s done,” Hadrian nodded. “Tell our fellow vessels to continue freeing the Potentia of our victims, order Martellus and his techmarines to commit to repairs as best they can, and get these filthy xeno corpses off my ship.”

He collapsed to his knees, leaving dents in the floor from the force of the motion.

“Until I am revived,” he continued as blood began to dribble out of his mouth in multiple streams, “Command is to be granted over to Martellus, as all things considered he is probably best for keeping the ships in as best condition as possible. Second command to you, third to Trexus.”

Finally, the enemy dead, their ships ruined in their hubris to come after a true challenge that he had offered, Hadrian fell forward onto his face and ceased to suppress the orders of his sus-an membrane. His body curled slightly as it fell into its coma as Nero stood over him, one hand still raised to ask another question that would go without answer. Then the Chief Apothecary looked around in the hallway, then back down to the Captain. Sighing again, he reached down and hefted the man up and began to drag him away.

“Marines like you remind me of Aramus sometimes,” he muttered, “Oh, I’m fine Nero, let me just run at the Greater Daemon, and the Hive Tyrant, and the Warboss! What’s that? Mortal wounds? You can fix me up can’t you, Nero?”

A twitch of his command vox and a few servo skulls came floating by.

“Tell Brother Julian that when I get there, the Captain is to be his full responsibility. I will take over for everyone else.”

They shot away as fast as they came.

“Oh no! I decided to keep hitting Araghast the Pillager while Cyrus set his bombs at our feet! Gordian’s gone, so now it’s up to you Nero!”

No one paid his muttering any mind as he went on his way. Thankfully for Captain Hadrian, he remained comatose for the entire journey.

Penetrating The Doom-Eater’s Innards
92 vs. 7

Long ago, at one point or another, the Blood Ravens had sought out a Primarch to be held up as their own amongst the gloried histories of all other chapters. This had happened multiple times. Once, when it appeared that some of their marines were afflicted with the Black Rage, the excitement that they might be of Sanguinius was exultant despite the issue that brought the possibility about. This was proven false, with bitter quiet anger on both sides when those mutated were ended. At another time, it was thought they could be of the Dark Angels, for some reason or another that none alive can recall. The stability and purity of their gene-seed was remarked upon for many a time. Another possibility was the Raven Guard, perhaps though this was a bit desperate in a search for connection by their shared heraldry. While all three were disproven or otherwise abandoned as theories, each time the Blood Ravens had marveled at the chance. Though it was gone now, it appeared, for just a time, that the Raven Guard might yet have some connection in some small way.

Cyrus would scoff at this, and say it was merely good scout work.

It was his scouts that found many a tunnel that led deeper in, found passageways that led to secondary reactors and supply depots. It was his scouts that, with the enemy so eagerly distracted with the louder thumping of the 1st​ Company, found weapons and vehicles aplenty. Staging areas were set with explosives to detonate as the orks marshaled themselves, flanking maneuvers while Captain Marcus drove the terminators further in. Occasionally, as the orks readied themselves for a charge against the 1st​ Company lines, they would be assaulted by shotguns and tossed grenades from behind. Hallways used to try and assault Marcus’ formations from the side found themselves collapsing.

They slipped as if living shadows deeper and deeper in the massive station, watching as the disheartened and disorganized orks began to butt up against one another.

Tracking The System Paths
5

The only problem, as it turned out, was that ork technology was incomprehensible. Completely so. Cable junctions led into the walls and then emerged unknowable locations elsewhere. Power stations ran dangerously hot but the actual direction of the energies sent were unknowable. Sometimes it looked like the orks had actually plugged generators into themselves for some reason, which to the consternation of the techmarines was somehow producing even more energy. Shunts and breakers were stuck into the walls at random intervals with the insane sensibilities of the ork meks. They pored over workshops and broken machinery, as well as the strange cybernetic implants of so many of the orks, but found little success.

The disruption pulse had fed in upon itself, turning the redlining generators and electrical systems in on one another. Larger systems, independent with their own generator systems, were somewhat unaffected. The guns on the outside of the station, for instance, perhaps its engines and certain shield production points. But without the system actually still running, there was no clear path to follow whatsoever. The benefits were obvious, they knew, but even so they were still struggling to find their way. All that was left was ‘deeper’, towards the core of the mixture of metal and stone. Where they found, of course, more and more orks. Some scouts posited that they simply needed to keep trying to find the largest concentrations. But the fighting was getting worse. As the hours stretched on, the biggest and strongest orks were beginning to properly assert themselves for the first time in years. Unrestrained chaos became, rather counter-intuitively, organized in so much as solidified groups were beginning to form.

“Triggering detonation packs!”

“Ork leader self-identifying as ‘Megaboss Krakskull’ has completed asserting his authority, his slugga and shoota boyz are starting to cut us off from proper reinforcement routes.”

“Neophyte Grant has lost the ability to use his legs, he will require an apothecary to put his bones back together. We are retreating currently.”

Tendrils of scouts were repulsed, twisted in upon themselves, and finally began to reverse themselves out of their own accord. The unyielding fist of the 1st​ Company found its progress slowed, though not entirely halted just yet. Elsewhere, upon a field of corpses, the other joined battlegroups stood unbroken. Some marines were injured, surely, but none more dead. Some would require rather significant healing, but for now their hearts thumped with still pulsing righteous fury. Cyrus, for his part, grit his teeth after setting off another series of explosives to collapse a ceiling onto another horde. He knew he was starting to run out of those supplied by the chapter. Improvised explosives were not something he was unfamiliar with, and it was not as if the orks did not have plenty of such things on their own.

The only way to push something as massive as the Doom-Eater through space was fuel and reactors in tremendous quantities. If they could not find Doom-Eater himself, perhaps they did not need to if they could simply destroy the entire superstructure. Main reactors which jumped and bounced in their housings. Fuel depots ready to be injected for that extra bit of speed. Giant cylinders that leaked where copious amounts of invented fuel mixtures sloshed. Internal factories where new weapons were manufactured. Control stations where gretchin and grots ran about to load ammo into the weapons that could damage and destroy even a battle barge. Big mek’s whose entire life’s work had been producing zzap kannons that could blast apart escort ships, each centered in their own little bubbles of influence and creation. Of course, there was also the idea of simply to keep pushing. To keep searching. The ork was possibly at the very, very center of the entire place. The only issue was actually getting there. The further into enemy territory, the more chance of risk without reinforcement. Of being surrounded and cut off. A dangerous proposition either way, for it was unknown what personal defenses and abilities the warboss might possess. Especially considering the size of the horde he was leading. On the other hand, if they could strip the stations' defenses enough, they might just be able to kill off the entire thing rather than continuing to risk themselves trying to find it's heart. Something to contemplate as he continued to isolate and kill as many orks as he possibly could.
