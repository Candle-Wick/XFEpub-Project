“In his name! In his name! In his name! In his name!”

The cultist died screaming that same line, repeated a hundred fold by his fellow damned. Men, women, children and elders, they all advanced in their filth-ridden robes with stinking half-dead bodies. The PDF fired into the masses charging forward at them from the uttermost depths of the underhive, yet such were their numbers and their impossible resilience that it took multiple lasbolts and salvos to put them down. Ahead at the cordon line, the elite members of the Arbites barred the way with power mauls and shotguns, ignoring the clawing at their bodies that was the most common weapon available to the cult. Some of their hands had rotted so badly that the tips of their finger bones had become exposed, sharpened to claws by time or effort both. Others threw their own filth as a weapon, sending those who had not properly sealed their suits or worn the masks as proscribed into coughing fits that became blood flecked and fatal before too long.

Ahead of them all, PDF and Arbites alike, was Razor Squad. The hab block’s quarantine efforts had been successful enough, for sure, but the source needed to be found more than anything else. The Blood Raven scouts slipped past groaning sentries with their fever burnt eyes, and over walkways slick with sick. The air itself was becoming corrosive, a mild discomfort to the space marines but something that could cause actual major harm to those less enhanced or protected. They did not even bother eliminating those in their way, finding it far easier to simply move past without being detected. They may have been scouts but by the reckoning of most they were more than worthy of promotion. Their sergeant, formerly of the 1st​ Company, would never attest to it, however. Not out of thinking otherwise, but rather preferring their experience and ability.

Newly promoted scouts, for instance, would never be trusted with such a mission from the start of their careers.

Silently, the target was found, hand signals more than enough to order the advance. An old, abandoned medical facility, it seemed. Not that there were many proper such places this far down, but rather it appeared that this place had been created by the community itself. A small bit of independent clinic work, innocents attempting to relieve their fellow man in a way that those who lived at the tops of the spires would never consider. It was a shame that it had failed so badly. It would never be known when those who ran it became corrupted, or why, but in the end, it didn’t matter. They had failed, and a place of healing had become quite the opposite to those with sane minds.

Sergeant Ankit blinked heavily as his eyes found the most glaring sign of just what had happened to that place.

It had been daubed in some disgusting mixture of bodily fluids and toxic waste products, but the symbol of Nurgle was unmistakable. More concerning was the fact it was glowing. Or rather, that the glowing was pulsing like some sort of heartbeat. The ground, metal and permacrete alike, was visibly rotting around the base of the building. There were more symbols around its walls, and likely more within. But it was more than enough. Still, he waited, pausing in his next instinct to call in the target coordinates for artillery bombardment as a pair of cultists emerged from the building. Both were monstrously swollen, skin peeling away in many places to reveal the feverish and maggot-ridden flesh beneath.

“They come…they come…,” one said, the same droning tone that Ankit had heard before.

Really, once their taint grew far enough, the cultists seemed to specifically get stuck into some single sentence or another.

“Yes…yes they do,” the other replied, the difference in tone and level of control, making Ankit tilt his head. “But so does Ulkair…so does he! His prison is a broken, mewling thing, and it cannot hold him forever….”

Then he turned, and Ankit reeled back as the needler rifle that had been hidden inside the hollow flesh of the cultist’s arm fired in his direction. The wall next to him was not so lucky. Even as he studied how the weapon was somehow hidden inside the arm – likely a result of bone turned rubbery and malleable along with musculature – he began firing back. The rest of the scouts around him did the same. Ankit was, most especially, glad that he had ordered everyone in Razor Squad to bring quadruple the usual amount of explosives. They would be needed.

=========================================
​
Gabriel exchanged a dark look with the rest of the Chapter’s leadership as Sergeant Ankit finished his report. The scout sergeant’s armor was scored and pitted, but he had managed to destroy the cult’s base without taking any casualties of his own. The PDF and Arbites had not been so lucky, but the cult had quite a horde of tainted humans on their side. The ensuing cullings that would be undertaken on Meridian would have to be exceedingly thorough, to the point that Governor Derosa had ordered the formation of another Meridian Regiment simply to partake.

“You did the Chapter proud, Ankit. If there is nothing else…?”

Ankit did not bother speaking in response, simply shaking his head.

“Very well.”

The communicator closed, leaving Gabriel to turn and look at his brothers. But words were not forthcoming, many simply thinking to themselves as to the threat that had unveiled itself. Gabriel had not personally been present for any of the battles on the planet Aurelia, but there were those present who had. Tarkus was not, but he had been there, and so there was little doubt that he would be at the least consulted on this matter. The traitor Avitus had been there as well, his bloody and body burnt with repeated applications of melta bombs upon its surface. Of Thaddeus and Aramus, there had not been word recently. Cyrus, however, merely looked pensive. It was Thule who spoke up instead.

“The Great Unclean One did say that his prison would not hold him for long,” Thule said gravely, “It seems that he was not exaggerating. We must contact the Ordo Malleus.”

“Would they bother coming?” Cyrus asked with a raised eyebrow. “We have not been on speaking terms much with them for some time now.”

Gabriel glared at the cynical Captain who shrugged in turn before looking towards the dreadnought.

“You are correct. The Grey Knights would not ignore a threat such as Ulkair breaking free and causing havoc once more. If his touch and influence is growing once more, then they must respond. I can speak with Inquisitor Toth, if he still lives, and you can speak to your contacts in the Grey Knights.”

It was difficult to make a dreadnought frame nod, but Thule was adept enough to manage it, his hull bobbing with the motion.

“Very well, Chapter Master.”

It would not happen immediately, the Librarius had not even reported major warp distortions within the subsector, but he would not ignore something like this. He could not. Nor could he ignore the demands of the Inquisition, or the issues remaining with Vandis. At the absolute least, the greenskins and eldar would not trouble the sector for some time. At least, he hoped that was true. The Blood Ravens were still growing in strength, and yet still their foes came on. They would never stop, he knew, the fighting would never end. It was his fate as a space marine. And yet, he knew no fear at such a thought. For he was a Blood Raven, and never again would his chapter be found wanting if he had anything to say about it.
